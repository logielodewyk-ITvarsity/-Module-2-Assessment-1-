WARTHOG

The common warthog is a wild member of the pig family found in grassland, savanna, and woodland in sub-Saharan Africa.

A warthog is identifiable by the two pairs of tusks protruding from the mouth and curving upwards.

The lower pair, which is far shorter than the upper pair, becomes razor-sharp by rubbing against the upper pair every time the mouth is opened and closed.

A tusk will curve 90° or more from the root, and will not lie flat on a table, as it curves somewhat backwards as it grows. The tusks are not used for digging, but are used for combat with other hogs, and in defense against predators – the lower set can inflict severe wounds.

The head of the common warthog is large, with a mane down the spine to the middle of the back. Sparse hair covers the body. Its color is usually black or brown. Tails are long and end with a tuft of hair.

Warthogs are powerful diggers, using both their snouts and feet.

Common warthogs will wallow in mud to cope with high temperatures and huddle together to cope with low temperatures.
